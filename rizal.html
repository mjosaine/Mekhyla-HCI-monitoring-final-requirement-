José Rizal

The National Hero of the Philippines

Biography

José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda (June 19, 1861 – December 30, 1896) was a Filipino nationalist, novelist, and key figure in the Philippine Revolution. Born in Calamba, Laguna, Rizal was the seventh of eleven children in a prosperous family. His mother, Teodora Alonso, was his first teacher and instilled in him a love for learning.

At age 9, Rizal was sent to Biñan for schooling before entering Ateneo Municipal de Manila in 1872, where he excelled academically. He later enrolled at the University of Santo Tomas to study medicine but faced discrimination from Spanish friars. This prompted him to continue his studies in Europe in 1882.

"The youth is the hope of our future." — José Rizal

Did You Know?

Rizal was a polymath who mastered 22 languages, including Latin, Greek, German, French, and Chinese. He was also an accomplished artist, sculptor, and fencer.

Education & Travels

Rizal's journey across Europe and Asia shaped his revolutionary ideas:

  • Madrid (1882-1885): Studied medicine at Universidad Central de Madrid, earning degrees in Philosophy and Letters and Licentiate in Medicine.
  • Paris (1885-1886): Specialized in ophthalmology under Dr. Louis de Wecker.
  • Heidelberg (1886): Studied under Dr. Otto Becker while working on his novel Noli Me Tangere.
  • Asia (1888): Traveled through Japan, the United States, and England, where he annotated Antonio de Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas.

Famous Works

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Rizal's first novel exposed the abuses of Spanish friars and colonial government through the story of Crisostomo Ibarra. Written in Berlin, it was published with the help of friend Maximo Viola.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

The darker sequel to Noli, published in Ghent, Belgium. It portrayed a more radical approach to reform through the character of Simoun.

Other Notable Works:

  • Mi Último Adiós (1896): His final poem, written before execution
  • To the Filipino Youth (1879): Award-winning poem
  • The Philippines a Century Hence (1889): Political essay predicting colonialism's end

Exile in Dapitan (1892-1896)

After returning to the Philippines in 1892, Rizal was exiled to Dapitan, Zamboanga for four years. During this period, he:

  • Established a school and hospital for the community
  • Developed a water system using clay pipes
  • Engaged in farming, fishing, and business ventures
  • Conducted scientific studies (discovered new species like the Draco rizali flying lizard)
  • Met Josephine Bracken, his companion until death

Martyrdom

Despite having no direct involvement with the Katipunan revolution, Rizal was arrested in 1896 while en route to Cuba as a volunteer doctor. He was:

  • Imprisoned in Fort Santiago, Manila
  • Tried by a military court for rebellion, sedition, and conspiracy
  • Found guilty and sentenced to death by firing squad

The Final Hours

On December 29, 1896, Rizal:

  • Wrote his masterpiece Mi Último Adiós, hidden in an alcohol lamp
  • Secretly married Josephine Bracken
  • Composed letters to family and his best friend Ferdinand Blumentritt

At dawn on December 30, 1896, Rizal was executed at Bagumbayan (now Rizal Park). His last request to face the firing squad was denied, but as he fell from the bullets, he twisted his body to die facing the sun - a final act of defiance.

Timeline of Rizal's Life

  • June 19, 1861: Born in Calamba, Laguna
  • 1872: Witnessed Gomburza execution; entered Ateneo
  • 1882: Left for Spain to continue studies
  • 1887: Published Noli Me Tangere in Berlin
  • 1891: Published El Filibusterismo in Ghent
  • 1892: Founded La Liga Filipina; exiled to Dapitan
  • December 30, 1896: Executed at age 35 in Manila
  • 1912: Declared National Hero by the U.S. colonial government

Legacy

José Rizal's impact continues today:

  • National Hero: Officially recognized as the Philippines' greatest hero
  • Rizal Day: December 30 is a national holiday
  • Monuments: Rizal Park, Rizal Shrines in Calamba and Dapitan
  • Currency: Featured on Philippine peso coins and bills
  • Education: Rizal's works are mandatory reading in schools
"I die without seeing the dawn brighten over my native land. You who have it to see, welcome it - and forget not those who have fallen during the night." — Last lines of Noli Me Tangere
