Andrés Bonifacio

The Father of the Philippine Revolution

Biography

Andrés Bonifacio y de Castro (November 30, 1863 – May 10, 1897) was a Filipino revolutionary leader and the supreme leader of the Katipunan, the secret society that ignited the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Known as "The Great Plebeian," Bonifacio rose from humble beginnings to become one of the most important figures in Philippine history.

Born in Tondo, Manila to Santiago Bonifacio and Catalina de Castro, Andrés was the eldest of six children. His parents died when he was young, forcing him to quit school and support his siblings by making and selling wooden canes and paper fans. Despite limited formal education, Bonifacio was self-taught, reading books about the French Revolution, biographies of US presidents, and novels like Victor Hugo's "Les Misérables" which influenced his revolutionary ideals.

"What matters death if one dies for what one loves, for native land and adored beings?"

— Andrés Bonifacio

Did You Know?

Bonifacio was multilingual, fluent in Tagalog and Spanish, and could understand some English. He worked as a clerk for a British firm, J.M. Fleming & Co., and later as a salesman for the German trading company Fressell & Co.

The Katipunan Revolution

On July 7, 1892, the same day José Rizal was exiled to Dapitan, Bonifacio founded the Katipunan (officially Kataas-taasan, Kagalang-galangang Katipunan ng mga Anak ng Bayan or KKK) with fellow patriots Ladislao Diwa, Teodoro Plata, and others. The secret society aimed for complete independence from Spain through armed revolution.

Bonifacio served as the Katipunan's Supremo (Supreme Leader) and developed its structure, rituals, and teachings. He authored several Katipunan documents including the "Decalogue" (Ten Commandments of the Katipunan) and the poem "Pag-ibig sa Tinubuang Lupa" (Love for One's Homeland).

In August 1896, after the Katipunan was discovered by Spanish authorities, Bonifacio and his followers tore their cedulas (community tax certificates) at Pugad Lawin in what became known as the "Cry of Pugad Lawin" or "Cry of Balintawak," marking the beginning of the Philippine Revolution.

Leadership and Contributions

As military leader, Bonifacio:

  • Organized and commanded revolutionary forces in Manila and surrounding provinces
  • Established a revolutionary government with him as President in 1896
  • Issued the "Katipunan Code" to govern revolutionary conduct
  • Designed the Katipunan flag featuring a sun with eight rays representing the first eight provinces to revolt

Bonifacio's leadership style was egalitarian. He believed in the power of the masses (the "plebeians") and rejected the elitism of the ilustrado class. His revolutionary government was based on the Katipunan's principles of equality, fraternity, and liberty.

Revolutionary Writings

Bonifacio was an accomplished writer who produced:

  • "Ang Dapat Mabatid ng mga Tagalog" (What the Tagalogs Should Know)
  • "Katungkulang Gagawin ng mga Anak ng Bayan" (Duties of the Sons of the Country)
  • Poems like "Pag-ibig sa Tinubuang Lupa" (Love for the Homeland)

Conflict with Aguinaldo and Tragic End

In March 1897, the revolutionary leaders held the Tejeros Convention to establish a new government. Despite being the Katipunan's founder, Bonifacio lost the presidential election to Emilio Aguinaldo. When Bonifacio's supporters protested electoral irregularities, violence erupted.

Aguinaldo's faction later arrested Bonifacio and his brother Procopio on charges of treason and sedition. After a controversial trial, they were sentenced to death. On May 10, 1897, Andrés and Procopio were executed in the mountains of Maragondon, Cavite.

"The revolution has no need of academic degrees; it needs ardent patriots!"

— Andrés Bonifacio's response to critics of his lack of formal education

Legacy and Honors

Though his life ended tragically, Bonifacio's legacy lives on:

  • Officially recognized as one of the Philippines' greatest national heroes
  • Featured on the Philippine 10-peso coin and 20-peso bill
  • Bonifacio Day celebrated every November 30 as a national holiday
  • Numerous monuments nationwide, most notably the Bonifacio Monument in Caloocan
  • Several cities, streets, and institutions named in his honor

Controversies and Historical Debates

Historians continue to debate:

  • Whether Bonifacio should be considered the first Philippine President
  • The fairness of his trial and execution
  • His exact role versus Aguinaldo in the Revolution
  • The location of the "Cry" that started the Revolution

Timeline of Bonifacio's Life

  • November 30, 1863: Born in Tondo, Manila
  • 1880s: Worked as a clerk and salesman while self-educating
  • July 7, 1892: Founded the Katipunan
  • August 1896: Led the "Cry of Pugad Lawin" starting the Revolution
  • March 22, 1897: Lost presidency to Aguinaldo at Tejeros Convention
  • April-May 1897: Arrested, tried, and sentenced to death
  • May 10, 1897: Executed in Maragondon, Cavite at age 33
  • 1918: First official recognition as national hero
